1. The Density Matrix & The Cut

In 1+1 dimensional QFT, the density matrix $\rho$ of a ground state can be represented as an unconstrained path integral over Euclidean (imaginary) time $\tau \in (-\infty, \infty)$. To find the reduced density matrix $\rho_A$ for a spatial subsystem $A$ of length $\ell$, we trace out the environment $B$.

Numerical Example: How Space is Cut

Imagine a 1D lattice of $M=6$ spins. Region $A$ contains spins $\{3, 4\}$, and Region $B$ is the rest $\{1, 2, 5, 6\}$. The total Hilbert space dimension is $2^6 = 64$.

Tracing out $B$ sums over identical bra and ket states of spins $\{1, 2, 5, 6\}$, giving $\rho_A$ of size $2^2 \times 2^2 = 4 \times 4$. In the path integral, the fields at $\tau=0^+$ and $\tau=0^-$ for $x \in B$ are forced to be equal (sewn together). For $x \in A$, they are left unconstrained, creating an open "cut" that acts as the row and column indices of $\rho_A$.

2. The Replica Trick

We compute the von Neumann entropy using the replica trick:

$S_A = -\lim_{n \to 1} \frac{\partial}{\partial n} \text{Tr}(\rho_A^n)$

We take $n$ identical copies of the path integral and glue them cyclically along the cut $A$. This forms an $n$-sheeted Riemann surface, $\mathcal{R}_{n,1}$. Notice how the 3D visualizer maps n=3 sheets.

3. Twist Fields & CFT

By mapping this multi-sheeted topology back to the complex plane, the branch points at the boundaries of $A$ correspond to inserting Branch-Point Twist Fields ($\mathcal{T}_n, \tilde{\mathcal{T}}_n$). Evaluating their correlation function gives:

$S_A = \frac{c}{3} \ln \frac{\ell}{a} + c'_1$

Global Quantum Quench

Suppose we prepare the system in a massive ground state $|\psi_0\rangle$ and unitarily evolve it with a critical CFT Hamiltonian $H$. This imparts an extensive excess of energy throughout the system.

Path Integral Representation

The time-dependent expectation value incorporates a small damping factor $e^{-\epsilon H}$ to make the path integral convergent. The density matrix becomes a path integral on a finite strip of width $2\epsilon$ in Euclidean time:

$\langle \phi'' | \rho(t) | \phi' \rangle = \frac{1}{Z} \int_{\text{strip}} \mathcal{D}\phi e^{-S_E[\phi]}$

Look at the 3D visualizer: The path integral sheets are now finite strips restricted between boundary states $|\psi_0\rangle$ at $\tau = \pm\epsilon$, analytically continued to $t$.

Quasiparticle Picture & Entropy

The initial state acts as a source of entangled quasiparticle pairs. As they propagate outward at velocity $v$, entanglement between $A$ and $B$ only occurs when one particle reaches $A$ and the other reaches $B$.

This leads to a linear growth followed by saturation at $t = \ell/2$:

$S_A(t) \simeq \begin{cases} \frac{\pi c}{6 \epsilon} t & t < \ell/2 \\ \frac{\pi c}{12 \epsilon} \ell & t > \ell/2 \end{cases}$

Local Quantum Quench

We physically cut a spin chain into two halves, prepare them independently in their respective ground states, and then join them together at $x=0, t=0$. The initial state has only a local difference from the ground state.

Path Integral with Vertical Slits

The physical cut corresponds to slits parallel to the imaginary time axis in the Euclidean path integral, representing the disconnected chains before $t=0$.

$\tau_1 \in (-\infty, -\epsilon], \quad \tau_2 \in [\epsilon, \infty)$

In the 3D visualizer: A vertical glowing red gap exists at $x=0$ outside the narrow region $|\tau| < \epsilon$, demonstrating the initially uncoupled nature of the halves.

Logarithmic Entanglement Growth

A local quench only produces a finite amount of energy and single localized excitation at $r=0$. The entanglement between the two halves (Region $A = (-\infty, 0]$, $B = [0, \infty)$) grows logarithmically over time:

$S_A(t) = \frac{c}{3} \ln \frac{t}{a} + const$

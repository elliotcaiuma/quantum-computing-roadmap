1. The Density Matrix & The Cut

In 1+1 dimensional quantum field theory, the density matrix $\rho$ of a ground state can be represented as an unconstrained path integral over imaginary time $\tau$. To find the reduced density matrix $\rho_A$ for a spatial subsystem $A$ of length $\ell$, we trace out the environment $B$.

Numerical Example

Imagine a 1D lattice of $M=6$ spins. Region $A$ is the interval containing spins $\{3, 4\}$, and Region $B$ is the rest $\{1, 2, 5, 6\}$. The total Hilbert space dimension is $2^6 = 64$. The full density matrix $\rho$ is a $64 \times 64$ matrix.

Tracing out $B$ means we sum over the identical bra and ket states of spins $\{1, 2, 5, 6\}$, leaving the reduced density matrix $\rho_A$ of size $2^2 \times 2^2 = 4 \times 4$. In the path integral, the states at $\tau=0^+$ and $\tau=0^-$ for $x \in B$ are forced to be equal (sewn together). For $x \in A$, they are left unconstrained, creating an open "cut" representing the row and column indices of $\rho_A$.

2. The Replica Trick

Calculating the von Neumann entropy $S_A = -\text{Tr}(\rho_A \ln \rho_A)$ directly is extremely difficult. Instead, we use the replica trick:

$S_A = -\lim_{n \to 1} \frac{\partial}{\partial n} \text{Tr}(\rho_A^n)$

To compute the trace of the density matrix to the $n$-th power, we evaluate the path integral over n=3 identical copies, glued together cyclically along the cut $A$:

$\text{Tr}(\rho_A^n) = \int \mathcal{D}\phi_1 \dots \mathcal{D}\phi_n \prod_{j=1}^n \langle \phi_j | \rho_A | \phi_{j+1} \rangle$

(with $\phi_{n+1} \equiv \phi_1$)

3. Twist Fields & CFT

The resulting geometry is an $n$-sheeted Riemann surface, $\mathcal{R}_{n,1}$. The curvature is zero everywhere except at the two endpoints of the interval $A$, where there are conical singularities.

By moving the multi-sheeted topology to the target space, the partition function becomes a correlation function of Branch-Point Twist Fields ($\mathcal{T}_n, \tilde{\mathcal{T}}_n$) in the standard complex plane:

$\frac{Z_n}{(Z_1)^n} = \langle \mathcal{T}_n(u)\tilde{\mathcal{T}}_n(v) \rangle$

The scaling dimension of $\mathcal{T}_n$ is dictated by the transformation of the stress tensor $T(z)$, yielding $d_n = \frac{c}{12}\left(n - \frac{1}{n}\right)$. Evaluating the correlation function and taking the $n \to 1$ limit gives the universal scaling law:

$S_A = \frac{c}{3} \ln \frac{\ell}{a} + c'_1$

Where $c$ is the central charge, $\ell = |u-v|$ is the interval length, and $a$ is the UV cutoff.
